Hallucination: an experience involving the apparent perception of something not present.

What is life as it happens? We take in signals, data really in different forms, from the world through our senses. Five senses, roughly, give or take. But when do these data points actually occur? We tend to think life happens instantaneously, but it doesn't. Everything that happens occurs just before it reaches our brains. The signal is always arriving from what was, a moment ago.

To go through the famous five senses:

  • Sight: EM wavelengths travel from various distances to our retinas, which turn into signals sent through the optic nerve to the visual cortex for processing; then finally input into our reality.
  • Hearing: sound waves hit our eardrum, which shakes three small bones, then makes disturbances in the cochlea, resulting in signals sent through the vestibulocochlear nerve, relayed through various sections of the brain before ending up in the auditory cortex; then finally input into our reality.
  • Touch: something presses on the sensors of our skin; the signal is sent from the location through peripheral nerves up through the spinal cord, through the brainstem and thalamus then up to the somatosensory cortex; then finally input into our reality.
  • Smell: We have olfactory receptor neurons in our nasal cavity. Signals travel a short distance along the olfactory bulb and tract to the olfactory cortex; then finally input into our reality.
  • Taste: While seemingly related to smell (if you lose your smell, how much do you taste?), it is quite different as far as the brain is concerned. Taste receptors are attached to three different nerves (cranial nerves 7, 9, and 10) through our more primitive brains up into the gustatory cortex; then finally input into our reality.

Sight and hearing are our long-range receptors. Smell is medium-range. Touch and taste are our short-range receptors. Still, they all take time to be processed. I suppose what we experience as life, happening instantaneously is when all signals comes together in the "present." What is the present though? A singularity? A limit that approaches but never resolves, like 1/x as x approaches zero?

In essence, life can be broken down into three distinct phases: data input, reaction, and memory. You could also frame these as future, present, and past. At the scale we live at, it all feels like the present. But break it down into infinitesimal chunks and what we are experiencing is already the past. We are hallucinating the present. Life is a controlled hallucination. The question is only what timescale you're measuring at.
